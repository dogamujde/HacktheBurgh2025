Postgraduate Course: The Arab-Israeli Conflict: Nations in Collision (PG Version) (IMES11073)

Course Outline

SummaryThe Arab-Israeli conflict stands as one of the most enduring and, some argue, most intractable political issues in the Middle East today. This course offers a critical examination of the history of struggle in Palestine between the Zionist movement and the local Palestinian population from its beginnings in the late Ottoman period until the present day, that has seen the establishment and expansion of the state of Israel, the ongoing dispossession and of the Palestinians, and a record of failed political solutions proposed to address the issue.

Additional PG-only classes

This course offers a critical examination of the encounter between the Zionist movement and the local population in Palestine, from its beginnings in the late Ottoman period until the present day. We will explore the changing relationship between these two agents and chart it as we cover the impact and legacy of the British mandate, the critical events of 1948 and 1967 that respectively saw the establishment and consolidation of the state of Israel and the continuing dispossession of the Palestinians, and the ongoing attempts to contrive a political solution. We will do this by investigating primary sources and secondary scholarship, both in text and audio-visual media, to analyse the terrain of a deeply politicised historiography that has been responsible for the established narrative and sought to critique it. Taught in conjunction with the Undergraduate Honours option, there will be additional PG-only classes.

Learning Outcomes
On completion of this course, the student will be able to:
  1. Ability to form an academically informed, independent and critical knowledge of the Arab-Israeli conflict
  2. Develop an in-depth understanding and knowledge of its dynamics through a detailed understanding of the conflict and a close examination of the main historical events, processes and actors that have shaped and continue to shape its dynamics in the Middle East
  3. Develop further ability to do secondary-source research.
  4. Present oral and written work clearly and effectively.
  5. Apply analytical skills to a reading of primary sources.
